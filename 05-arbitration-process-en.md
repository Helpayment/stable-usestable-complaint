# How I Plan to File Arbitration Against Stable (www.usestable.com)

> This article documents how I am preparing to pursue arbitration against Stable / useStable / www.usestable.com. It is not legal advice. It is a practical record for non-U.S. users who may face similar problems with a U.S. virtual address or virtual mailbox provider.

Stable’s Terms of Service at <https://www.usestable.com/terms> identify **Mistro, Inc. dba Stable** and reference arbitration. If I continue with formal dispute resolution, arbitration may become a necessary step.

Official links:

- Stable Terms of Service: <https://www.usestable.com/terms>
- Stable website: <https://www.usestable.com/>
- AAA Consumer Rules, Forms, and Fees: <https://www.adr.org/rules-forms-and-fees/consumer/>

## Step 1: Build a Timeline

Before filing arbitration, I will organize the facts into a clear timeline:

- when I registered and bought Stable / useStable services;
- when I saw www.usestable.com pricing and help center pages;
- when the passport was mailed and later destroyed;
- when Stable requested an upgrade;
- when Stable closed or refused to restore the account;
- when payments failed or were refunded;
- when I contacted Stable support, risk, and management emails;
- when I warned that I would prepare arbitration;
- what remains unresolved, including mail in transit, address migration, and billing descriptors.

## Step 2: Organize Evidence

I will group evidence into:

- Stable Terms of Service, pricing pages, help center pages, and purchase screenshots;
- passport handling and destruction records;
- “unlimited recipients” and forced upgrade communications;
- account closure notices and risk review messages;
- billing records showing donations-like descriptors;
- refund and dispute communications;
- business impact records such as address dependencies and mail risk.

Public versions will be redacted to protect private information.

## Step 3: Send a Formal Dispute Notice

Before arbitration, I will send a formal dispute notice to Stable / useStable / www.usestable.com. It will explain who I am, what services were involved, what I believe Stable did wrong, what evidence I have, and what resolution I seek.

My requested remedies may include:

- explanation of passport handling;
- restoration or a real transition period;
- paid forwarding or handling of mail in transit;
- explanation of recipient limits and upgrade rules;
- explanation of the Stable Companions Donations billing descriptor;
- compensation for reasonable losses caused by sudden closure and document handling.

## Step 4: Use AAA Rules and Forms

AAA provides consumer arbitration rules, fee schedules, demand forms, and fee waiver forms. I will review the current AAA forms and determine whether my claim should be filed as a consumer, small business, or another category.

For a non-U.S. user, I will pay special attention to fee waiver options, filing fees, and whether the arbitration clause applies to my situation.

## Step 5: Make the Process Public

I plan to document each stage:

- when the dispute notice was sent;
- whether Stable replied;
- how evidence was prepared;
- what AAA requested;
- how fees or fee waiver issues were handled;
- whether Stable offered settlement;
- what obstacles appeared.

The goal is not to harass Stable. The goal is to show that users outside the United States do not have to silently accept account shutdowns, template replies, and refunds that do not solve the real operational damage.
